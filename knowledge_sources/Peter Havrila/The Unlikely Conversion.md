# Peter Havrila and the Unlikely Conversion

Peter Havrila, a curious white European boy with an aptitude for mechanics and history, never intended to alter the course of the twentieth century. His time machine—assembled from scavenged electronics and a stubborn belief that causality could be negotiated—was meant only for observation. History, he believed, should be studied, not touched.

The machine deposited him in Moscow in the late 1940s, beneath a gray sky heavy with ideology. After days of careful maneuvering, Peter found himself face to face with Joseph Stalin. The dictator was smaller than Peter expected, quieter too, his eyes sharp with suspicion.

Peter did not argue politics. He did not speak of power or empires. Instead, he spoke of accountability, of moral agency, and of a faith that emphasized repentance, family, and the capacity for personal change. He spoke calmly, without accusation, framing belief not as surrender, but as discipline.

Stalin listened.

What followed was not a public announcement or a sudden transformation of the state. It was private, incremental, and almost invisible. Stalin requested texts. He asked questions. He challenged assumptions. Peter answered what he could and admitted what he did not know.

When Peter returned to his own time, history books remained unchanged. Yet, somewhere in the margins of the past, a man known only for iron resolve had, for a brief moment, contemplated humility.

Peter shut down the time machine and documented nothing. Some changes, he decided, were not meant to be proven—only attempted.
